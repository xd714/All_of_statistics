Chapter 1: Introduction to Statistics

Mathematical Foundations and Key Concepts

üéØ What is Statistics?

Statistics is the science of collecting, analyzing, interpreting, presenting, and organizing data. It provides tools to make sense of uncertainty and variability in the world around us.

üåü Real-World Example

Medical Trial: Testing a new drug's effectiveness by comparing treatment outcomes between two groups - one receiving the drug, another receiving a placebo. Statistics helps us determine if observed differences are due to the drug or just random chance.

üìä Types of Data

Quantitative vs Qualitative

  • Quantitative (Numerical): Height, weight, income, test scores
  • Qualitative (Categorical): Gender, color, brand preference

Discrete vs Continuous

  • Discrete: Countable values (number of children, dice rolls)
  • Continuous: Any value in a range (height, temperature)

üé≤ Dice Rolling Example

Roll a fair die many times:

  • Individual rolls: Uniform distribution {1,2,3,4,5,6}

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Misconceptions

‚ùå "Correlation implies causation"

Strong correlation doesn't prove causal relationship. Ice cream sales and drownings are correlated (both increase in summer), but ice cream doesn't cause drowning!

‚ùå "Larger samples are always better"

Quality matters more than quantity. A well-designed small study can be more valuable than a poorly designed large study.

‚ùå "Statistical significance = practical importance"

p < 0.05 doesn't necessarily mean the effect is large or practically meaningful. Always consider effect size!
